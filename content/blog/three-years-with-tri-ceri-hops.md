I can't believe it's been almost three years.

When we started this journey with Loki, the outlook was grim. We were told to hope for six to nine months post-surgery. We were preparing for a goodbye; instead, we got a transformation. Today, this boy is going as strong as ever, proving that sometimes, the "experts" don't know who they're dealing with.

## The "Trip" That Wasn't

It started about three years ago with what looked like a clumsy "dad moment"—Loki tripped coming up the deck stairs. He limped, he shook it off, and we didn't think much of it. Weimaraners are basically land-sharks made of rubber and chaos, right?

But a few days later, the "chaos" took a dark turn. His joint swelled. He stopped putting weight on the leg. By the time we called the vet, a lump was growing with terrifying speed. Our happy, driven boy became lethargic and stopped eating. He wasn't himself; he was a ghost of a dog.

The vet took one look and the air left the room. The X-ray was haunting—cancer had literally eaten the bone away. To make matters more stressful, the tests for the specific type of cancer came back inconclusive. We were flying blind against an invisible enemy.

## Rolling the Dice

We were devastated. We had not were not prepared for this news, and the options felt like a "choose your own adventure" book where every ending was sad: chemo, amputation, or ending his suffering entirely.

After a lot of research, and late-night talks, we decided to take a gamble. We rolled the 20-sided die and opted for amputation. The vet warned us: this could be like cutting the head off a Hydra. If the cancer decided to come back with a vengeance which is common it will show up in his lungs, and we'd be out of options.

## The Level-Up

The day of the surgery, we were absolute wrecks. We were mourning the dog he used to be, not sure how or what he would be like post surgery.

Then the phone rang.

"He's out of surgery," the vet said. "And he's... well, he's currently hopping around the office like a maniac."

Wait, what?

Not only did we land a Natural 20 on that surgery roll, but Loki apparently rolled a 100 on his "save" vs. death through. We dropped off a depressed, dying dog that morning and picked up a three-legged speedster who acted like he'd just shed some dead weight. He got home, greeted his best friend Belle, and immediately started "worming" around the floor. My buddy was back.

## The Legend of the Tri-Ceri-Hops

Fast forward three years. Loki is a blur of silver fur and flying ears. Watching him "run-hop" is a masterclass in resilience—and a bit of comedy.

Does he have a bit of an attitude? Oh, absolutely. He's not the "run up and lick a stranger" type. He values his personal space and isn't afraid to let his "inner brat" out when people stare too long at the Tri-Ceri-Hops. But that's just Loki. He's lived long enough to be a grump if he wants to be.

He's a reminder that even when the odds are stacked against you, sometimes you just have to hop right through it.
