We've all heard of the [3-3-3 rule](/adoption) for rehoming a dog. It's a lovely, organized guideline. But Paige? Paige doesn't do "guidelines." Paige operates on a proprietary temporal rift known as Weim-Time.

When Paige first joined us, she wasn't exactly looking for a BFF. She trusted no one, viewed a leash as a personal insult (transforming instantly into a 90lb bucking bronco), and had exactly one method of communication: The Varied Chomp.

## The Dragon and Her Hoard

Early on, I discovered Paige's secret identity: she is actually a small, grey dragon. During bird training, she decided that downed quails weren't for "retrieving"—they were for "hoarding." She would pile them up and stand guard like Smaug over his gold.

In those moments, you have a choice. You can go for the correction, or you can look into those stubborn eyes and realize how much that "prey" means to her. I chose to wait. I chose to build a bridge instead of a wall.

## The PB&C Breakthrough

Fast forward 1.5 years. This past hunting season, something clicked. Paige pointed a Woodcock perfectly, retrieved it like a pro, and presented it to me. I was ready to retire and buy her a steak dinner.

And then… she found the deer leg.

If you've never seen a Weimaraner claim a skeletal remain, it's a sight to behold. It was a firm "MINE. NOT YOURS, DAD. NEVER YOURS."

Most would say, "Take it away! Correct her!" But I took a different approach. I sat down in the dirt next to my bone-chewing dragon and pulled out my secret weapon: Peanut Butter Crackers.

I didn't try to bribe her. I didn't try to snatch it. I just sat there, ate a cracker, and petted her while she gnawed on her prize. For the first time, she didn't bolt. She didn't "chomp." She just… stayed.

Then, I put my hand out and said the magic word: "Release." This was a HUGE Gamble, if she failed to "Release" I would have to correct her….

And she did.

## The Long Game

We spent the next 15 minutes playing "Pass the Gross Deer Leg." She'd give it to me, I'd give it back, she'd get a pet, and we'd repeat. She realized I wasn't there to steal her joy; I was there to share it.

It took 18 months to get to that deer leg epiphany. It wasn't an overnight transformation, and we still have "work in progress" stamped on our foreheads. But seeing her hunt for us and not just for herself? That's the human (and canine) spirit in a nutshell.

Trust isn't built in three days, three weeks, or even three months. Sometimes, it's built one peanut butter cracker and one crusty deer leg at a time.
